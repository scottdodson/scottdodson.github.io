04.24.2025 - My early technology career began in 1994. I was working as a black and white photo finisher at Ivey Seright in Seattle, Washington. I was something of a computer hobbyist at the time. I put together an Intel machine to run early versions of Linux, built a LAN to play network games, and setup access to the World Wide Web. This suite of skills gave me an opportunity to work at Zombie Studios, one of the first Virtual Reality game studios. I took a contract position with Microsoft in 1996 to do level design. The one title that was published with my work was Close Combat 2. I worked on maps and developed the procedure for creating foliage and rubble. In 1997 I worked on the design an unpublished title for Microsoft. It was an adventure game in a persistent world.

Conceiving of and documenting the design of a whole game proved to be unwieldy. There are so many interrelated parts it was difficult to keep their relationships straight. One weekend while taking a break from design documentation I watched Robert Zemeckis' Contact. There is a scene that illuminates if a problem defies understanding in two dimensions, try three. The following insight has been useful to me professionally.

Three dimensions represent volume. The simplest regular solid is a tetrahedron. What if I thought of whole experience of a software product as the volume defined by the relationship of four categories. These are the ones I used.

  • the World - objects and physical rules of a game environment
  • the Toy - mechanisms the player uses to interact with the game world.
  • the Game - rules that govern success, failure, reward, and consequence.
  • the Story - representative content contextualizing the World, Toy, and Game.

By thinking of a tetrahedron defined by these four points, one can conceive the whole of video game existing within a boundary. Thinking about relationships is simplified. Is the Toy fun? If we change the Toy, how that that impact the other elements? The Toy/World relationship is a sandbox that runs without rules.

In 1998, I shifted my career focus to web infrastructure, taking a position with Getty Images. There I applied interdimensional relationship ideas to the task of infrastructure and service delivery. I went on to develop and support services at Drugstore.com, Perkins Coie LLP, Zonar System, and Amazon Web Services. In 2019 I decided to pursue a degree in Computer Science. I graduated from the University of Washington in 2023.
